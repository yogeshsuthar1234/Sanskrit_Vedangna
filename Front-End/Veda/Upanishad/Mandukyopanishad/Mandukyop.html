Mandukya Upanishad

Exploring the profound wisdom of one of the shortest yet most significant Upanishads

Understanding the Mandukya Upanishad

The Mandukya Upanishad, despite being the shortest of all Upanishads with just 12 verses, is considered one of the most profound texts in Vedantic philosophy. It belongs to the Atharvaveda and focuses on the mystical significance of the sacred syllable "Om" and the four states of consciousness.

This ancient text provides a comprehensive explanation of the self (Atman) and its relationship with the ultimate reality (Brahman). The great philosopher Gaudapada wrote an extensive commentary on this Upanishad, and Adi Shankaracharya considered it sufficient for liberation.

"This syllable Om is indeed Brahman. This syllable is the Highest. Whosoever knows this syllable obtains all that he desires."

— Mandukya Upanishad, Verse 1

The Four States of Consciousness

1

Vaishvanara (Waking State)

The first quarter is Vaishvanara, whose sphere of activity is the waking state, who is conscious of external objects, who has seven limbs and nineteen mouths, and who enjoys the gross objects of the material world.

2

Taijasa (Dream State)

The second quarter is Taijasa, whose sphere of activity is the dream state, who is conscious of internal objects, who has seven limbs and nineteen mouths, and who enjoys the subtle objects of the mental world.

3

Prajna (Deep Sleep State)

The third quarter is Prajna, whose sphere is deep sleep, in whom all experiences become unified, who consists of bliss, who enjoys bliss, and who is the doorway to the knowledge of the dream and waking states.

4

Turiya (Transcendental State)

The fourth state is Turiya, which is not conscious of the internal world, nor of the external world, nor of both together. It is not a mass of consciousness, nor is it simple consciousness, nor is it unconsciousness. It is unperceived, unrelated, incomprehensible, uninferable, and indescribable.

The Mystical Syllable "Om"

The Mandukya Upanishad explains that the syllable "Om" represents both Brahman (the ultimate reality) and the four states of consciousness:

  • A

    The sound "A" represents Vaishvanara (the waking state)

  • U

    The sound "U" represents Taijasa (the dream state)

  • M

    The sound "M" represents Prajna (the deep sleep state)

  • ∅

    The silence after "Om" represents Turiya (the transcendental state)

Historical Context

800-500 BCE

Estimated period when the Mandukya Upanishad was composed, during the later Vedic period when the major Upanishads were being formulated.

6th-7th Century CE

Gaudapada wrote his famous commentary (Karika) on the Mandukya Upanishad, which became a foundational text for Advaita Vedanta philosophy.

8th Century CE

Adi Shankaracharya wrote a commentary on both the Mandukya Upanishad and Gaudapada's Karika, establishing its central importance in Advaita Vedanta.

Modern Era

The Mandukya Upanishad continues to be studied worldwide and has influenced various spiritual traditions, including modern mindfulness practices and consciousness studies.
